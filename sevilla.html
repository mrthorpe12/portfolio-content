Sevilla and Andalucia

A day's train ride from Madrid is Andalucia, the southernmost region of Spain. Conquered by Moorish kings from North Africa in the 8th century A.D., the region has a unique flair that combines Spanish Catholic culture with Islamic architecture. Sevilla, the capital of Andalucia, offers picturesque alleys, buildings, and squares. Peacocks roam in the city's gardens and parks, while at the heart of the old city is the Real Alcazar, former home of the Moorish rulers and today a prominent landmark that draws toursits from across the globe.[1]

From Sevilla, one can take a day trip to the fortress town of Granada, home to what is perhaps the region's most famous attraction - La Alhambra. Begun in 1238 by Muhammad I Ibn al-Ahmar, then the ruler of Granada, the fortress-palace was built in stages until it was seized during the Christian invasion of Andalucia, a period known as the Reconquista. Following the Reconquista, the Alhambra became home to the royal court of Spain. It was here that Christopher Columbus received the endorsement of Spanish monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella when planning his expedition to Asia. Today the site is world-renowned, as is the town of Grenada.[2]

From Grenada, it is possible to visit the more rural parts of Andalucia, including the region's famous "hill towns". Built to defend Catholic settlements in Andalucia during the Reconquista, today these fortified outposts are known for their stunning whitewashed buildings and cobbled streets. Stop for a glass of sangria and enjoy the sweeping vistas which many of these towns command.[3]
